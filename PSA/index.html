1. How much should I bet ?

Are you in for some betting ? Let’s play a game. There are some specific rules to this game: A fair coin will be flipped until it falls on its head for the first time. You will be paid $2 to the power of j dollars if this occurs on the jth toss. You are sure to win at least 2 dollars so you should be willing to pay to play this game, but how much ? Few people would pay as much as 10 dollars to play this game. See if you can decide, by simulation, a reasonable amount that you would be willing to pay, per game, if you will be allowed to make a large number of plays of the game. Does the amount that you would be willing to pay per game depend upon the number of plays that you will be allowed ?

note: St. Petersburg paradox

2. I bet my life on this one

Are you still in the mood for betting ? Now let’s play something more interesting and serious! Let's try our skill at Russian roulette. Here's the conditions:
I only have two bullets in my revolver, which has a 6-slot barrel. Now I put the bullets into the revolver in adjacent slots, spin the barrel and hand you the gun. You point the gun to your head. You pull the trigger and ... Click! you're still alive. Congratulations but the game is not over yet. You have to pull the trigger one last time. Now you have two choices:

  1. You spin the barrel afterwards you pull the trigger.
  2. You pull the trigger without spinning.
Luckily you have some time and a computer with you in your bag so you can simulate the current situation such that you can choose the correct choice.
Your assignment is to calculate the probability for both cases and after you've found the probability for the initial conditions you need also to find out what are the probabilities in case the bullets are not adjacent.
Now you need to calculate the same probabilities but in case when you have 2 bullets and the gun has a 5 slots barrel. You have to present the result for 8 different outcomes. Good luck staying alive.

  • 6 slots, adjacent bullets, spin barrel
  • 6 slots, adjacent bullets, do not spin
  • 6 slots, non-adjacent bullets, spin barrel
  • 6 slots, non-adjacent bullets, do not spin
  • 5 slots, adjacent bullets, spin barrel
  • 5 slots, adjacent bullets, do not spin
  • 5 slots, non-adjacent bullets, spin barrel
  • 5 slots, non-adjacent bullets, do not spin
3. Labouchere strategy

One of the most popular casino games is roulette. Many people like roulette because it is an easy game to learn and play. Unlike other casino games, roulette doesn't have many complicated rules to follow or strategies to perfect. Because it's so simple, many people have tried to invent different strategies to win this game. One method is the Labouchere system. It is played as follows:
write down a list of numbers, usually 1, 2, 3, 4. Bet the sum of the first and last, 1+4=5, on red. If you win, delete the first and last numbers from your list. If you lose, add the amount that you last bet to the end of your list. Then use the new list and bet the sum of the first and last numbers (if there is only one number, bet that amount) in the roulette game. Continue until your list becomes empty. Show that, if this happens, you win the sum 1+2+3+4 = 10, of your original list.
Simulate the system and see if you do always stop and, hence, always win. If so, why is this not a foolproof gambling system ?

4. Oscar's Grind Strategy

Another well-known gambling strategy is the Oscar’s Grind (also known as Hoyle's Press), one conservative method designed to withstand long losing streaks. This system is based on the idea of breaking your session down into "sequences," where the specific goal of each sequence is to make exactly 1 unit of profit. It is played on even-money bets (like red/black or even/odd) as follows: Start with an initial bet of 1 unit. If you lose, your next bet remains exactly the same as your previous bet. If you win, increase your next bet by 1 unit, unless doing so would result in a sequence profit of more than 1 unit. In that case, you only bet the exact amount needed to achieve exactly 1 unit of total profit. When you reach +1 unit of profit, the sequence ends. You pocket the profit and start a new sequence with a 1-unit bet. Simulate the system and assume that you are betting on black. Evaluate: how the total profit/loss and your bet sizes evolve over time during a long sequence of alternating streaks of wins and losses, and why or why not this strategy might fail to guarantee profits, despite the initial logic.

1. Interesting observation

2. National Election

In an upcoming national election for the President of the United States, a pollster plans to predict the winner of the popular vote by taking a random sample of voters and declaring that the winner will be the one obtaining the most votes in his sample. Suppose that 48% of the voters plan to vote for the Republican candidate, and 52% plan to vote for the Democratic candidate. To get some idea of how reasonable the pollster's plan is, write a program to make this prediction by simulation. Repeat the simulation 100 times and see how many times the pollster's prediction would come true.

Repeat the experiment assuming a 49%/51% split. Also try with a sample of 3000 instead of 1000.

3. Densities (Stick Breaking)

Take a stick of unit length and break it into two pieces, choosing the breakpoint at random. Now break the longer of the two pieces at a random point. What is the probability that the three pieces can be used to form a triangle?

Write a computer program that would simulate the experiment. Explain your results.

4. Densities (Circle Quadrilateral)

Four points are chosen at random on a circle of unit circumference. What is the probability that the quadrilateral formed by these points is convex and that all its interior angles are less than 120 degrees?

5. Family cases

Let's estimate, by simulation, the average number of children there would be in a family if all people had children until they had a boy (Scheme 1). Do the same if all people had children until they had at least one boy and at least one girl (Scheme 2). How many more children would you expect to find under the second scheme than under the first in 100,000 families?

6. Densities (Coin Toss Checkerboard)

At the Ziua Vinului, a coin toss game works as follows. Coins of 25 bani are tossed onto a checkerboard. The management keeps all the coins, but for each coin landing entirely within one square of the checkerboard the management pays 1 leu. Assume that the edge of each square is twice the diameter of a coin, and that the outcomes are described by coordinates chosen at random. Is this a fair game?

7. Densities (Heads Histogram)

Write a program to carry out the following experiment. A coin is tossed 100 times and the number of heads that turn up is recorded. This experiment is then repeated 1000 times. Have your program plot a bar graph for the proportion of the 1000 experiments in which the number of heads is n, for each n in the interval [35, 65]. Does the bar graph look as though it can be fit with a normal curve?

8. Densities (Circular Table Neighbors)

At a mathematical conference, 10 participants are randomly seated around a circular table for meals. Using simulation, estimate the probability that no two people sit next to each other at both lunch and dinner. Can you make an intelligent conjecture for the case of n participants when n is large?

9. Random Variables (Uniform Game)

A game is played as follows: A random number X is chosen uniformly from [0, 1]. Then a sequence $Y_1, Y_2, \dots$ of random numbers is chosen independently and uniformly from [0, 1]. The game ends the first time that $Y_i > X$. You are then paid $(i - 1)$ dollars. What is a fair entrance fee for this game?

10. Important Distributions (Jora Petrovici)

Jora Petrovici never pays the troleibuz (cost = 7 lei in Chisinau). He assumes that there is a probability of 0.05 that he will be caught by the controlor. The first offense costs 50 lei, the second costs 200 lei, and subsequent offenses cost 300 lei. There's also an empirically proven probability of 0.02 that the taxatoarea is a hairy muscular guy, which means that he'll have to pay the 7 lei anyway. How does the expected cost of riding the troleibuz for a year (two times a day) without paying compare to the cost that is paid by us, law-abiding students?
